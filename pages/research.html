Research

Soft materials exhibit many types of disorder, from impure crystals to glasses to elongated chains. I use numerical simulations to study patterns of disorder and phase changes in a variety of soft systems.

Colloids on Patterned Substrates

Colloids on patterned substrates can form well tailored grain boundaries separating ordered, crystalline regions. Several of those patterns are shown in the image, consisting of a square patterned substrate of traps, covered with a varying number of colloids annealed to the groundstate. The image is colored by a Voronoi tessellation, where gray indicates ordered hexagonal crystalline regions (i.e. particles with six neighbors), dark blue are square regions (particles with four neighbors). Stripes of red and light blue are grain boundaries, indicating particles with either five (light blue) or seven (red) neighbors. Red more about this fun and fascinating system here:

Domain and Stripe Formation Between Hexagonal and Square Ordered Fillings of Colloidal Particles on Periodic Pinning Substrates , D. McDermott, J. Amelang, L. Lopatina, C.J. Olson Reichhardt, C. Reichhardt, Soft Matter 9, 4607 (2013).

Active Matter flowing across disorder

Self-propelling agents consuming energy from the environment are inherently non-equilibrium physical systems. With Pacific University undergraduates, I'm exploring how active matter responses in complex environments. The onset of swarming in active matter is an order-disorder phase transition that can be detected with statistical measurements of clustering. Biological and synthetic active matter exhibit collective behaviors which are modified by environmental disorder. A herd of sheep clogs while moving through a bottleneck. Free E. coli exhibits run-and-tumble motion,3 but confined E. coli moves by directed hopping in a porous media. Engineered active colloidal rollers transition from collective flow to disordered non-directional motion due to scattered environmental obstacles.

In the past, I studied ratchet effects in active matter, including reverse ratchet effects where the active particles had a net backward flow due to collective effects.

Collective ratchet effects and reversals for active matter particles on quasi-one-dimensional asymmetric substrates, D. McDermott, C.J. Olson Reichhardt, C. Reichhardt, Soft Matter 12, 8606 (2016).

Machine Learning

How can we predict the onset and characteristics of non-equilibrium phase transitions? Machine learning algorithms can extract patterns from large datasets by building predictive mathematical models when given the proper data inputs. The intersection of machine learning and statistical mechanics offers promising tools to classify disorder in soft matter. I'm working on new ways to classify dynamical disorded systems, building on the work described in the link below:

Detecting Depinning and Nonequilibrium Transitions with Unsupervised Machine Learning, D. McDermott, C.J.O. Reichhardt, C. Reichhardt, Phys. Rev. E 101, 042101 (2020).
